🌍 Tournament Overview — Did You Know?
◆
Record-Breaking Scale

The 2026 World Cup is the largest in history — 48 teams, 104 matches, 39 days across 16 stadiums in 3 nations.

◆
Three-Nation First

USA, Canada & Mexico are the first trio of nations to jointly host a World Cup. The idea was proposed as early as 2016.

◆
Expanded Format

The new format features 12 groups of 4. The top 2 from each group, plus the 8 best third-place teams, advance to a round of 32.

◆
Trophy Tradition

The original Jules Rimet Trophy was stolen in England in 1966 (and found by a dog named Pickles). The current FIFA World Cup Trophy weighs 6.175 kg and is made of 18-carat gold.

◆
Host Record

The USA previously hosted in 1994, setting an attendance record of 3.59 million fans — still unbeaten. Mexico hosted in 1970 and 1986.

◆
Stadium Icon

The Estadio Azteca in Mexico City becomes the first stadium to host matches at three different World Cups: 1970, 1986, and 2026.

⭐ Records & Legends — The Numbers
◆
Brazil's Unbroken Run

Brazil is the only nation to have played in all 22 World Cups. They've also won more than any other team (5 times) and are the only team to have scored in every edition.

◆
Fastest Goal Ever

Turkey's Hakan Şükür holds the record for the fastest World Cup goal — just 11 seconds into the 2002 third-place match against South Korea, a goal that stood as the record for over 20 years.

◆
Klose's Record

Germany's Miroslav Klose holds the record for most World Cup goals: 16, scored across 4 tournaments (2002–2014). He's the only player to score in 4 consecutive tournaments.

◆
The Pelé Triple

Pelé remains the only player to win three World Cups (1958, 1962, 1970) — and he scored in every single one. He was just 17 when he won his first.

◆
Most Titles

Germany and Brazil share the record with 4 and 5 titles respectively. Argentina, France, Italy, and Uruguay have each won it twice or more.

◆
Biggest Victory

Hungary beat El Salvador 10–1 in 1982 — the biggest margin of victory in World Cup history, though Hungary still didn't advance from their group.

🏟️ Venue Spotlight — The Stadiums
◆
The Final Venue

The MetLife Stadium in East Rutherford, NJ — seating 82,500 — will host the 2026 World Cup Final on July 19. It's the largest stadium in the NFL and one of the biggest in North America.

◆
The Azteca Legacy

Estadio Azteca hosted two of the greatest matches ever played: Brazil 4–1 Italy (1970 final) and Argentina's 'Hand of God' and 'Goal of the Century' games in 1986.

◆
SoFi Stadium

Los Angeles' SoFi Stadium — home of the Rams and Chargers — opened in 2020 at a cost of $5.5 billion, making it the most expensive stadium ever built.

◆
AT&T Stadium

AT&T Stadium in Dallas features the world's largest cable-supported roof structure and two of the world's largest HD video boards — each larger than the playing surface itself.

◆
Hard Rock Miami

Hard Rock Stadium in Miami has undergone a $550 million renovation. The open-air canopy keeps fans shaded while keeping out the summer Florida rain — crucial for a June tournament.

◆
Canada's BMO Field

BMO Field in Toronto, expanded for 2026, will host the opening match between Canada and Bosnia — making it an extraordinary moment for Canadian football history.

🆕 Debut & Returning Nations — New Faces
◆
Curaçao's Miracle

Curaçao (pop. 160,000) is one of the smallest nations ever to qualify for a World Cup. Their journey through CONCACAF qualification was one of football's great underdog stories.

◆
Uzbekistan Breaks Through

Uzbekistan becomes the first Central Asian nation to reach a FIFA World Cup, qualifying through the AFC — a landmark moment for football in the former Soviet republics.

◆
Jordan's Debut

Jordan appears in their first World Cup, having only narrowly missed out multiple times before. Their 2023 Asian Cup final run proved their readiness for the global stage.

◆
Scotland's Long Wait

Scotland returns to the World Cup for the first time since 1998 — ending a 28-year drought. They've qualified 8 times total, but have never advanced beyond the group stage.

◆
Haiti's Second Time

Haiti last played in a World Cup in 1974 in West Germany. Their qualification via CONCACAF is a testament to the region's growing football quality beyond just Mexico, USA, and Canada.

◆
Iraq's 40-Year Gap

Iraq last played in a World Cup in 1986. Their qualification through the AFC ends a 40-year absence and is one of football's most remarkable stories of perseverance.

🔥 Greatest World Cup Upsets — Shock Results
◆
USA 1 – 0 England (1950)

The United States beat England — considered one of the favourites — in the 1950 World Cup in what is still called 'The Miracle on Grass.' The goal was scored by Haitian-born Joe Gaetjens.

◆
West Germany 3 – 2 Hungary (1954)

Hungary were unbeaten in 4 years and scored 25 goals in 5 WC games — yet lost the final to West Germany 3–2. Known as the 'Miracle of Bern,' it's one of sport's greatest shocks.

◆
Algeria 2 – 1 W.Germany (1982)

Algeria beat the reigning European Champions and 3-time World Cup winners in Spain 1982 — one of football's first great African shocks. It directly inspired a generation of African nations.

◆
Cameroon 1 – 0 Argentina (1990)

Cameroon — 10 men — beat defending champions Argentina in the opening game. Roger Milla's celebrations around the corner flag became iconic.

◆
Saudi Arabia 2 – 1 Argentina (2022)

Coming back from 2-0 down to beat the South American champions and pre-tournament favourites. Saudi Arabia triggered a national holiday and global disbelief.

◆
Japan 2 – 1 Germany (2022)

Alongside their win over Spain, Japan became the first team to beat two former World Cup champions in a single tournament group stage. A tactical masterclass from Moriyasu.

📊 World Cup By The Numbers — Facts & Figures
◆
Group Stage Complete

All 72 group stage matches have now concluded. From here, 32 teams advance to the knockout rounds — the largest Round of 32 in tournament history.

◆
Time Zones

Matches were broadcast across time zones from Pacific (PT) to Central European Time. With 16 host cities, no single fan timezone was perfect — yet every match found a global audience.

◆
Continental Breakdown

2026 features: UEFA (16), CONMEBOL (6), CONCACAF (8), CAF (9), AFC (8), OFC (1) — reflecting a bold expansion of global football representation.

◆
Prize Money

FIFA has set the total prize fund at over $1 billion for 2026 — the highest in the competition's history, reflecting the commercial scale of a 48-team tournament.

◆
The Final Venue

On July 19, 2026, two teams will meet at MetLife Stadium in New Jersey for the greatest prize in football — in what could be the most-watched sporting event in human history.

◆
The Next Chapter

The 2030 World Cup will celebrate the centenary of the competition, with matches across Spain, Portugal, Morocco, and three centenary matches in Uruguay, Argentina, and Paraguay.
